Etnia Negra





The Black ethnic group refers to people of African origin who share cultural, historical, and genetic ties to the African continent. In Brazil, this population has had a strong presence since colonial times, when millions of Africans were forcibly brought to work as enslaved laborers. Despite the cruelty of this process, Black people resisted and made a profound contribution to the development of Brazilian identity, especially in music, cuisine, religion, and language.

Black culture is rich and diverse, reflecting the many ethnic groups and nations of origin within Africa. In Brazil, this heritage is seen in cultural expressions like samba, candomblé, capoeira, maracatu, and many others that are now part of the national identity. African influence is also present in the way people speak, their customs, and traditional foods such as acarajé, feijoada, and other dishes rooted in African traditions.

Despite their cultural and historical importance, Black communities still face social challenges like structural racism, economic inequality, and violence. That’s why it's essential to recognize the value of the Black ethnic group, fight prejudice, and promote inclusion and appreciation of diversity. Celebrating Black culture and fighting for equity is not only a matter of justice, but also of honoring the rich history and cultural wealth that shape Brazil.

Black leaders, artists, and thinkers have played key roles in shaping not only Brazilian society, but global culture as well. Figures such as Zumbi dos Palmares, a symbol of Black resistance in Brazil, and international icons like Martin Luther King Jr., Nelson Mandela, and Maya Angelou have inspired generations through their fight for justice, equality, and human rights. Their legacy continues to influence movements that promote racial equality and social awareness.

Black culture is rich and diverse, and its influence is seen in music, food, religion, and traditions around the world.
